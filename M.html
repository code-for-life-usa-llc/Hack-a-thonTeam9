MEDICINE

KEY TECHNOLOGIES AND APPROACHES

Foundation and language models (LLMs): Automate clinical notes, summarize complex medical histories, and act as diagnostic copilots for physicians. High-precision computer vision: Analyzes X-rays, MRIs, and CT scans in seconds, detecting tumors invisible to the human eye. Human digital twins: Virtual patient replicas that allow for pre-surgical simulations and the assessment of bodily reactions to medication. Intelligent medical robotics: Operating rooms featuring autonomous robots for high-precision suturing and cutting, as well as microbots that travel through the bloodstream. Continuous biometric sensors: Smartwatches and patches with integrated AI that monitor vital signs and alert medical teams to heart attacks or health crises before they occur.

Although the COVID-19 pandemic drove and popularized the use of telemedicine and digital health in general, researchers state that it remains an integral part of healthcare. Telemedicine is particularly noteworthy for both its accessibility and its commercial implications. Many private practices use telemedicine to operate on a larger scale and even to open the door to new revenue streams, such as online subscription models offering 24/7 access to care. In 2024, the number of patients worldwide consulting their doctors online surpassed 116 million, nearly doubling the 2019 figure of 57 million. As we move through 2025 and look to the future, physicians will likely continue to leverage this trend, integrating telemedicine to reach and treat patient populations that would otherwise be inaccessible.
